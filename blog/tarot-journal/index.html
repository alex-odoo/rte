How to Keep a Tarot Journal: Track Your Readings and Growth

A tarot journal is the single most powerful tool for developing your reading skills. Here is exactly how to start and maintain one.

Why Journaling Transforms Tarot Practice

A tarot journal does three things that no other learning tool can replicate. First, it creates a personal database of readings that reveals patterns invisible in any single session. Second, it trains you to articulate symbolic insights in words — a skill that separates casual card-flippers from genuine readers. Third, it provides feedback: when you compare your morning interpretation with what actually happened, you calibrate your reading accuracy in real time.

Most tarot readers who stall in their development share one common trait: they do not journal. They read books, watch videos, and pull cards — but without writing down their impressions and reviewing them later, the learning dissolves. A journal makes each reading permanent and cumulative.

"Your tarot journal is not a record of what the cards said. It is a record of what you learned about yourself through listening to them."

What to Record in Every Entry

Keep the daily entry format simple enough to sustain indefinitely. Here is the essential structure:

The Core Fields

  • Date — Day of the week matters for pattern tracking
  • Moon phase — Note it with a symbol. Over months, you will discover fascinating correlations. See our moon phases and tarot guide
  • Question asked — Write the exact question. Question quality shapes reading quality
  • Card(s) drawn — Card name, upright or reversed, and position in spread
  • First impression — One to two sentences capturing your gut reaction before consulting any reference. This is the most valuable field — it trains intuition
  • Interpretation — Your full reading, synthesizing intuition and card knowledge

Optional But Powerful

  • Emotional state — How were you feeling before the reading? This affects interpretation
  • Spread used — Single card, three-card, Celtic Cross, etc.
  • Imagery details — What specific visual elements caught your attention?
  • Evening reflection — How did the card's theme show up during the day? This is where the deepest learning occurs
  • Accuracy rating — On a 1-5 scale, how relevant was the reading? Track this and watch your accuracy climb over months

A Sample Daily Entry

Here is what a complete daily journal entry looks like in practice:

March 25, 2026 — Tuesday — Waning Gibbous

Question: What do I need to know about today?
Card: IX — The Hermit (upright)
First impression: Solitude feels right. I need to work alone and trust my own lantern today.
Interpretation: The Hermit suggests withdrawing from social noise to find clarity. Today is for deep work, not networking. Trust my own expertise rather than seeking external validation.
Mood before: Slightly anxious about a deadline

Evening update: Incredibly accurate. I cancelled a lunch meeting, worked alone all afternoon, and produced the best work I have done in weeks. The anxiety dissolved once I stopped trying to multitask socially.
Accuracy: 5/5

This entire entry took under five minutes to write. The evening update took two. Total investment: seven minutes for a day's worth of tarot learning.

Weekly and Monthly Reviews

The real power of a tarot journal emerges when you review it at longer intervals. Schedule these reviews:

Weekly Review (5 minutes)

  • Which cards appeared this week? Any repeats? Repeated cards signal persistent themes
  • What was the ratio of Major to Minor Arcana? Major-heavy weeks indicate significant life periods
  • Which suits dominated? This reveals where your energy is focused
  • What was your average accuracy rating?

Monthly Review (15 minutes)

  • What was your most-drawn card this month? This is your "card of the month" — it carries a message worth sitting with
  • How did your accuracy change from the first week to the last?
  • Were there correlations between moon phase and card type?
  • What personal meanings have you developed that differ from textbook definitions?
  • What zodiac-related patterns do you notice?

After six months of journaling, readers commonly discover that certain cards have acquired deeply personal meanings that are more accurate for them than any published interpretation. These personal associations — born from lived experience — are your most valuable tarot asset.

Physical vs Digital Journaling

Physical Journal

Pros: The ritual of handwriting deepens engagement. No screen distractions. Many readers find that insights flow more freely through a pen than a keyboard. A beautiful journal becomes a sacred object in your practice.

Cons: Not searchable. Harder to track statistical patterns. Cannot be backed up easily.

Digital Journal

Pros: Fully searchable — find every time you drew The Tower in seconds. Enables statistical tracking (which cards appear most, accuracy trends). Easy to include photos of your readings. Backed up automatically.

Cons: Screen-based. Lacks the ritualistic quality of handwriting. Easier to skip.

The Hybrid Approach

Many experienced readers use both: a quick digital log for daily entries (a spreadsheet or notes app) and a dedicated physical journal for significant readings, monthly reviews, and deeper reflections. This combines digital searchability with handwritten depth.

Getting Started Today

The best moment to begin journaling is right now. Here is your minimal viable start:

  1. Open any notebook or notes app
  2. Write today's date
  3. Draw one card — from your deck or from Daily Tarot
  4. Write your first impression in one sentence
  5. Tonight, add one sentence about how the card's theme appeared during the day

That is it. Two sentences. You have started a tarot journal. Build from there — add fields as they feel useful, not because a guide told you to. The practice belongs to you.

Pair your journal with a daily tarot practice for maximum skill development, and incorporate tarot meditation sessions on weekends for deeper entries.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a special journal for tarot?
No. Any notebook or notes app works. Choose whatever you will actually use daily. The format matters less than consistency.
What should I write in my tarot journal?
At minimum: date, card drawn, question asked, and first impression. The optional evening reflection — how the card's theme appeared during the day — is where the deepest learning happens.
How long should each entry be?
For daily entries, 3-5 sentences is enough. For significant readings, write as much as feels natural. Quality of observation matters more than quantity of words.
Should I use a physical journal or digital?
Both work. Physical offers ritual depth; digital offers searchability and stats. Many readers use a hybrid: quick digital daily log + detailed physical journal for significant readings.
